Foundation · the insurance analogy

Selling a put is like selling insurance.

Forget the trading jargon for a moment. The wheel rests on a single concept that everyone already understands: insurance. Once that clicks, every other piece of vocabulary — strike, premium, assignment — falls into place.

The setup

Imagine someone owns shares and is worried.

A trader holds a position in the S&P 500 index. They're worried about a sudden drop. They'd like protection — a guarantee that if the market falls below a certain level, they can sell their position to someone at that level, no matter how far things have fallen.

They're willing to pay for that protection. Like buying insurance on a car. The premium is small relative to what they're protecting.

Your role

You agree to be the insurer.

You agree: "If the market falls below 7,180 by next week, I'll buy your position at 7,180 — no matter where the market actually is."

In return, they pay you a premium upfront — say, $200. That money is yours immediately. The promise is what you've sold; the premium is what you've collected for selling it.

The vocabulary

The strike

The threshold price. In insurance terms: the floor below which the policy pays out. In our example: 7,180.

The premium

What they pay you to take on the obligation. Yours immediately, regardless of what happens next.

The contract

The MES futures contract — what you'll have to buy if the market falls below the strike. Each MES point = $5.

Expiration

When the policy ends. If the market hasn't fallen below the strike by then, the obligation simply expires.

Two outcomes — that's it

Either the policy is never used, or it gets called in.

Outcome A · the common one

The market stays above 7,180. The policy expires unused. You keep the $200. Then you write another policy. This is the wheel's bread and butter — pure income, no position to manage.

Outcome B · the assigned one

The market falls below 7,180. The policy is called in. You buy the contract at 7,180 — even though it's worth less now. You still have the $200 premium. Now you own the position and the strategy shifts to selling it back at a profit.

So what actually happens after you sell the put? Two things. Only ever two.
Story · part one

Path A: the simplest outcome.

In the world where the market behaves, the wheel is almost embarrassingly simple. You sell a put, the put expires worthless, and you keep the premium. Then you do it again. And again.

Why does this work?

Because most days, the market does not move enough to breach a slightly out-of-the-money strike. The premium you collect is the market's payment for taking that small probability of assignment off their hands. Over hundreds of cycles, the math compounds.

But not every day behaves. So what happens on the days that don't?
Pause · what do you think?
If MES closes below 7,180 at expiry — what happens to the trader?
That's right. The premium is yours regardless — that money was paid upfront when the put was sold and it never goes back. What changes is that you now own the contract: the buyer of the put exercises their right to sell MES to you at 7,180. You become a holder of one MES contract at a price you agreed to in advance.
Story · part two

Path B: assignment. The wheel turns.

When the market closes below your strike, the put is exercised. You take delivery of one MES contract at 7,180. This is not failure — this is the wheel doing what it was designed to do. Now the strategy shifts from collecting put premium to managing a real position.

Path B · Assignment

MES finishes below 7,180 at expiry

The put is in the money. Assignment happens automatically. You now hold one MES contract at 7,180. The premium of $200 is still yours, sitting in your account.

Effective cost basis: 7,180 strike − 40 pts premium = 7,140. As long as you eventually exit at or above 7,140, the cycle is profitable. The premium effectively reduces what you "paid" for the position.

Assignment is part of the income mechanism — not a bug. The wheel converts assignments into the next phase of the strategy.

What now?

You hold the MES contract. You wait for price to recover to a level where covered calls make sense. Then you sell calls and collect more premium until the contract is eventually called away — and capital returns to cash.

How to read the ladder

The horizontal bands are the three zones the price can be in after assignment. The dark line is where MES is right now.

Above 7,180 — clean exit zone

Price has fully recovered above the assignment level. Covered calls make sense. The next call assignment closes the cycle profitably.

7,108 to 7,180 — call window zone

Price has recovered enough that selling covered calls becomes meaningful. Premium is collectible, exits are realistic.

Below 7,108 — recovery wait

Price is too far below the assignment to sell calls efficiently. The strategy waits — collecting nothing — until price returns to the call window.

The 7,108 readiness level is 99% of the assignment strike — empirically the point where covered call premium becomes worth the position cap.

Pause · think about it
When the wheel sells a covered call, what's it actually hoping for?
Right — exercise is the goal. The wheel doesn't sell covered calls just to collect premium. It sells them as the exit mechanism for the assigned position. When the call is exercised, the MES contract is sold at the strike, the cycle closes, capital returns to cash, and the wheel is ready to sell a fresh put. Repeated covered calls without exercise just mean the position keeps lingering — useful for income but not for closing the loop.

A short break for the most important sentence in the whole document.

The wheel doesn't beat the market by being right about direction — it beats the market by being patient about premium.

Patience requires capital. So how much do you actually need? Here's the empirical answer.
Capital sizing · why 40%

Capital decides whether you survive the worst day.

The wheel only fails permanently if you're forced out of a position before recovery. The amount of capital you reserve per contract decides whether that ever happens. The 40% recommendation isn't arbitrary — it comes from 36 years of S&P 500 history.

Step 1 · What "notional" means

The full size of the position you'd own if assigned.

If you sell a put at strike 7,180 on MES (Micro E-mini S&P 500), and you get assigned, you take on a futures position worth 7,180 × $5 = $35,900. That figure is the notional — the full market value of what you'd be holding.

You don't need to put up that much to trade — futures use leverage. But during recovery, the position's mark-to-market loss is calculated against this full notional. The capital you reserve has to absorb whatever drawdown the worst few days might produce.

Why 40% specifically

The worst single non-crash drawdown observed in S&P 500 daily data from 1990 through 2026 was −18.4% from a recent high. A 40% capital reserve absorbs that move plus a margin buffer, ensuring the position is never force-closed during normal market stress. During genuine crash periods (2000–2002, 2008, 2020, 2022), WISE blocks new entries entirely via its VIX hard stop and event gates — so the 40% reserve is calibrated to non-crash conditions, where the strategy actually operates.

Capital is the floor. WISE is what builds on top of it — turning the wheel from a manual strategy into a disciplined, auditable system.
Where WISE fits · the engine

The wheel is the strategy. WISE is the engine that runs it.

A trader can run the wheel manually — pick a strike, watch the chart, decide when to exit. WISE turns each of those judgment calls into a scored, auditable, data-driven decision. Same strategy. Different discipline.

01 · Strike scoring

Every put candidate gets ranked.

Most tools use a fixed delta band or a set distance from spot. WISE ranks every available strike on the live chain by variance-risk-premium-adjusted edge, liquidity, survivability, and phase-specific penalties — and surfaces one recommendation.

02 · Phase awareness

Assignment isn't a failure — it's a phase change.

When a put becomes a long MES position, WISE switches engines automatically: the put-scoring engine retires, the call-scoring engine activates, cost basis is recorded, and recovery state is tracked through every covered-call cycle until the position closes.

→ FROM WISE: "Phase: COVERED CALL. Cost basis 7,140. Best call: 7,200 strike. Carry: positive. Recovery 4 of 12 days."
03 · Named risk gates

Refusals are auditable, not vague.

WISE runs tiered risk gates: a VIX hard stop at ≥30, half-size at 28–30, FOMC day hard block, slow-recovery freeze, and event-tier blocks. When a trade is blocked, WISE names which gate stopped it — not a generic "conditions not met."

The honest part · risks

The real challenge is time under pressure.

The wheel collects premium reliably — but premium is not the same as profit. Three risks define what can actually go wrong, with figures from 36 years of SPX history.

Risk 1

Premium is income, not a guarantee.

You collect the premium upfront, every cycle, no matter what. But if assignment goes deep and recovery takes time, the position's mark-to-market loss can outweigh many cycles of premium collected.

Worst single assignment, 36yr−18.4%
Risk 2

Recovery can take time.

Median recovery from a put assignment to the call window is about 3 trading days. But the 80th-percentile case takes 12 days. And in the worst 1.6% of cases, the position has not recovered within 120 trading days.

80th pct recovery (put side)12 days
Risk 3

Capital matters.

During recovery, the account holds the contract through unrealised drawdown. Without enough capital to absorb the mark-to-market move, the position can be force-closed before recovery — turning a temporary loss into a permanent one.

Glossary · the words you need

Plain definitions, no jargon.

MES contract

The Micro E-mini S&P 500 futures contract. One point = $5.

Put option

A contract you sell to collect premium in exchange for accepting assignment risk below the strike.

Strike

The price written into the option contract. In our example: 7,180.

Premium

The cash collected when the option is sold. Yours regardless of outcome. Example: 40 pts = $200.

Assignment

The moment the put is exercised — you take 1 MES at the strike. Premium stays with you.

Covered call

A call sold against an MES contract you already hold. The MES "covers" the obligation.

Cost basis

Strike minus premium. Example: 7,180 − 40 = 7,140. Exit at or above this and the cycle is profitable.

Call window

The price zone where covered calls become productive. WISE uses 99% of strike.

Called away

The MES contract is sold at the call's strike. Cycle complete. Capital returns to cash.

The shortest version

Sell. Wait. Collect. Repeat.

Sell a put. If it expires (about half the time), keep the premium and start again. If you're assigned, hold the MES contract, sell covered calls until you're called away, and return to cash. That's the whole strategy. WISE is what turns it from a manual judgment call into a scored, auditable, gated decision.
